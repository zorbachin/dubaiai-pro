## The Amazon Ring

We were traveling on a budget. I was a guy who had lost his income six months earlier, and somehow I had to buy a ring without tipping her off.

I know the proper way to do it. Learn her size, her style, all of it. But I do not like to ruin a surprise. So I guessed. Style, cut, size. And I did it the most modern old-fashioned way there is. I bought it off Amazon.

It arrived while we were staying at a friend's place in LA. Of all the packages that showed up that trip, that was the one Meytal noticed. Thankfully she is not the suspicious type, and I order enough in the few weeks I am in America that the boxes tend to blur together. This one caught her eye. I made something up, and we moved on.

I proposed at the Duomo. She liked the ring. It did not fit, and it was not horrible.

That could have been the whole story. A budget ring, a yes, a good memory. But the ring had a second half, and I did not know it yet.

## Diamonds for Adir

Back in Israel, I found work launching a media channel called Moral Edge. It is doing really well now. [link: Moral Edge]

One of the projects I took on was a documentary about October 7. Seven families whose children were taken hostage or whose lives were taken, and how each of them turned the worst thing imaginable into something that saves other lives.

I met Jen Airley early on. She told the story of her son Binyamin, who died a hero in the war. She reminded me of my mom. My brother's death was not a violent one, but the loss a parent carries for a son who was full of life gives off the same frequency, and I felt it sitting across from her.

Jen told me about Alon Mesika, and his Diamonds for Adir program. So I called him.

If anyone could prove that Israelis are the warmest, most genuine people on earth, it is Alon Mesika. A strong head of hair, strong hands, and the warmest way about him of anyone in the Tel Aviv diamond exchange. Before we had even met in person, his words were simple. Any time. I am here.

His son Adir was at the Nova festival and never made it home. Adir had been about to get engaged. Since he would never get that chance, Alon, a jeweler himself, made a Facebook post. He would donate a diamond to a soldier who was getting engaged.

One diamond became ten. Ten became hundreds. To keep his son alive in the world, Adir is now part of all of these new marriages, all these new homes, built in his honor. [link: Diamonds for Adir]

I wear Adir's hat and his necklace, the personal brand he had wanted to launch.

## Made by Hand

When I told Alon on the phone that I was getting married and in the market for wedding rings, he told me about his workshop. You come in, you get sized, and then you come back and make the rings yourselves. I would make hers. She would finish mine.

While we were there, two soldiers walked in and told harrowing stories from combat, and stories they had heard about Adir. Some of it had to stay off camera.

It was heavy. It was powerful.

And then, at our wedding outside Jerusalem, the rings on our hands were ones Meytal and I had actually shaped and formed, with all of that history behind them. That was really special.

## More Than a Ring

From Amazon to handmade. From a ring I guessed at to one we built together, carrying the weight of a decision to move to Israel in the middle of a war with no job, of working on something that keeps a person's memory alive, and of watching the very beginning of a mission start to come true.

It stopped being a ring. It became a symbol, and a sign that things were changing.

And maybe that says something about the first half too. It was never about having everything finished and perfect. Sometimes an Amazon ring that does not fit at first is exactly what you need, so that later you can make the real one by hand.

Here is what I am actually using this space for, at this stage. Accountability. Reflection. And motivation.

Motivation is contagious for the people who are looking for it, and repulsive to the people who are afraid of it. I have a hard time being around people who lack it or reject it. And sitting with families whose lives were shattered, who took that and turned it into something that gives life, and now carrying a piece of that on my hand in permanent form, is a quiet kind of fuel.

A ring is a signal of who you are, and who you are becoming.
