What Drives the Price Up or Down

No two garages are exactly alike. Here's what your actual contractor considers when building your quote:

1. Surface Preparation

This is the biggest variable — and the one shady contractors skip to offer a low number. Diamond grinding (the right way to prep concrete) opens the pores so the coating bonds chemically, not just mechanically. Shot blasting is the alternative for larger commercial jobs. Acid etching — what the $899 guys use — barely does anything. Budget contractors skip prep almost entirely and use a thin, water-based coating that looks fine for six months and then peels.

Proper diamond grinding adds $0.50–$1.00 per square foot to the job. A 500 sq ft garage that costs $400 extra to prep correctly is the difference between a 2-year floor and a 20-year floor. Never let a contractor skip it.

2. Coating Type and Thickness

100% solids epoxy vs. water-based epoxy vs. a "garage floor paint" — these are completely different products. A lot of what's marketed as "epoxy" is a water-based coating with 20–40% solids content. It's thin, it's cheap to apply, and it fails quickly. Professional installations use 100% solids epoxy at 100% mix ratio, laid down at 10–12 mils thick. That's what the lifetime warranty is built on.

3. Flake Coverage

Full-broadcast flake (where excess flake is thrown until rejection and then scraped back) gives you 100% coverage and a textured, anti-slip surface. Partial-broadcast — where the contractor throws a handful and calls it done — looks thin and patchy. Full broadcast costs slightly more material but produces a significantly better floor. Ask any contractor what coverage percentage they use.

4. Top Coat

A polyaspartic top coat adds $0.75–$1.25 per square foot but dramatically extends the life of the floor. It handles Austin's UV exposure without yellowing (epoxy alone will amber over time), it resists tire marks and chemicals better, and it cures harder. If a quote doesn't include a top coat, ask why — then be skeptical.

5. Crack and Pit Repairs

Standard cracks and spider-web shrinkage cracks are common in Austin-area concrete, especially in Cedar Park, Round Rock, and newer construction in Georgetown where slabs settle. Most contractors price minor crack repair separately — typically $75–$200 depending on severity. Significant structural cracks may require consultation before coating.

6. Stairs, Curbs, and Add-Ons

Step edges, curb lips, and built-in work benches all add labor time. Expect $50–$150 for a standard step set. Safety flake borders, cove bases, and drain surrounds are priced individually.

Why $899 Quotes Are a Red Flag

Be Careful

We get calls every month from Austin homeowners who hired someone based on a $699 or $899 Facebook Marketplace quote. By year two the floor is peeling. The "contractor" is unreachable. And now they're paying us to diamond-grind everything off and start over — which costs more than doing it right the first time would have.

Here's the math on why $899 is impossible for a quality installation:

  • Diamond grinding rental or amortized machine cost: ~$200–$350
  • Professional-grade 100% solids epoxy (2-gal kit for a 1-car): ~$120–$180
  • Color flake (full broadcast on 250 sq ft): ~$60–$100
  • Polyaspartic top coat (2-gal kit): ~$150–$220
  • Labor (4–6 hours for a 1-car, skilled installer): ~$300–$500

That's $830–$1,350 in materials and labor alone on a small single-car job before any margin. The only way to hit $899 is to use inferior materials, skip prep, or shortchange labor. All three of those choices result in a floor that fails.

Legitimate contractors in the Austin market are pricing standard 2-car jobs at $1,800–$2,600. That's the real market. Anyone quoting meaningfully below that is either cutting corners or planning to hit you with "surprise" upcharges once they're already in your driveway with their equipment.

Is Epoxy Worth It in Austin?

At $1,800–$2,600 for a 2-car installation, epoxy is one of the best dollar-for-dollar home upgrades available in Austin. Consider what you get:

  • A floor that looks better than 95% of garages on your block
  • Concrete protected from oil, chemicals, and moisture for 15–20+ years
  • A dramatically easier garage to keep clean (sealed surface, not bare porous concrete)
  • Meaningful ROI on home resale — especially in Westlake, Round Rock, and Cedar Park where garages are large and buyers notice
  • A floor your family can actually use — for hobbies, a home gym, a workshop

Compare that to replacing carpet in a 500 sq ft room ($2,000–$3,000 for mid-grade), or a simple bathroom remodel ($5,000–$15,000). The garage floor delivers more visible transformation per dollar than almost anything else in the home.

How to Get the Most Accurate Quote

The best way to get a real number is to have a contractor come measure and assess your slab. But you can get very close with our online calculator — input your garage dimensions and coating preference, and we'll give you a tight range instantly.

When comparing quotes from multiple contractors, ask each one:

  • What prep method do you use — acid etch, shot blast, or diamond grind?
  • What percentage solids is your epoxy?
  • Is a polyaspartic or polyurea top coat included?
  • What does the warranty cover, and is it transferable?
  • Is this the final price, or are there common add-ons after you arrive?

A contractor who answers those questions clearly and without hesitation is probably legitimate. One who deflects, says "it depends," or doesn't know what percentage solids their epoxy is — walk away.
